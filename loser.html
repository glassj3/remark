Having Anxiety Doesn't Make Me a Loser

by Jon Westenberg

Having Anxiety Doesn't Make Me a Loser

by Jon Westenberg

BE WHO YOU ARE

And it doesn’t make you a loser. I was called a loser this week. By a tech bro who was pissed that I decided not to be a co-founder in his startup because I didn’t have the emotional capital for it. I’ve known him for a long time. And his response to my honesty was to lash out.  

But that response is something I’ve heard before, will hear again, and don’t let myself become affected by. Every now and then, I turn into a clenched, twisted, angry, biting ball of pure stress and anxiety. And the chances are, so do many of you.

We’re eaten away at by this hot and burning sensation that’s equal parts fear, anger, panic and insecurity. It’s bloody hard to be productive when that’s happening, let me tell you. You can’t write a marketing email when you’re so stressed and anxious that you can’t think straight. Ditto for getting clients,

making sales, writing blog posts, producing videos… You see people extolling the virtues of the endless hustle, and you feel bad about what you’re doing, and how much you’re struggling, because your hustle can only be 70%, about 90% of the time.

And then you think, maybe I’m not meant to be doing this, because maybe real entrepreneurs don’t get anxiety, or maybe real entrepreneurs don’t struggle with this shit or maybe if I don’t get it together, I’ll never make it… So what’s anxiety, and what some signs that you suffer from it as an entrepreneur?

Here’s some of the signs, from This Way Up: Excessive worrying that lasts for months, plus some or all of the following: Feeling restless, keyed up, or on edge. Having difficulty concentrating, or having your mind go blank.

Being irritable. Having tense or sore muscles. Having difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or having restless, unsatisfying sleep. Over-planning Excessive list making Seeking reassurance from others

I know that each and every one of these, I deal with on a daily, weekly or monthly basis. Don’t feel like you can’t admit it, don’t feel like you have to hide from it. Be open and honest about it with yourself. I am willing to admit that anxiety and depression have been a large part of my daily process for around 15 years now.

…and that anxiety makes the following difficult to achieve: Getting out of bed Getting dressed Staying focused Managing tasks Meeting goals everything else…

But there is nothing — and I mean nothing — wrong with being an entrepreneur who is dealing with this kind of shit. It doesn’t mean you can’t make it, and it doesn’t mean you won’t make it, and it doesn’t mean anything more than that you’re a human being. The trick isn’t to “manage” it, it’s to find ways to work within and without it that can let us actually get shit done! This is one method that has been hugely effective for me:

Be an observer. Watch your thoughts and feelings without engaging with them.

Karen Young: Anxiety has a way of drawing you in and making you engage with every anxious thought that comes in thinking distance of you. It’s exhausting! Experiment with standing back and watching your thoughts as an observer, knowing that when they are ready, they will pass. Sometimes we need to engage with thoughts and feelings,

and sometimes we need to stand back and wait for them move on. Try imagining your thoughts and feelings as a bubble, and then watch them float by. Experiment with letting them be, without needing to change them, understand them, or talk yourself out of them.

Imagine them hovering in the air around you, without becoming a part of you. Just let them be, without holding on too tightly. When they are ready to go, let them go. Think of it like this — rather than standing in the middle of a thunderstorm, trying to change the direction of the wind, imagine yourself watching that storm through a window, knowing that it will pass.

Try: ‘There’s a thought about what might happen if it rains on the holiday. Look at that. Didn’t know that was there.’ ‘A feeling about going to the interview. Interesting.’

Do not feel bad about who you are, just because you have crap to deal with.

It’s okay to be in this position. I don’t let anyone make me feel bad about who I am and what I’m dealing with. I have people ask me why I’m not scared to lose contracts or credibility by admitting and being this open about my personal struggles; well frankly, if my openness makes someone not want to do business with me, they’re not somebody I want to do business with either!
