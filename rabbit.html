Out of the Rabbit Hole

by Adam T. Cummings, CEO of SIUVA

Out of the Rabbit Hole

by Adam T. Cummings, CEO of SIUVA

TRUST YOURSELF

Will there ever be a day in the future for which will afford me the opportunity to get off this emotional roller coaster I call my life? I am not referring to me no longer living. I am, more or less, referring to the reaching of a level of self-growth that will no longer need fits of depression as a way of evolving into a better version of myself.

I have dealt with manic depression in one form or another for most of my life. My ability to mask myself around people has always kept the inquisition of my mental state unnecessary. I tend to hide in plain sight. My genuine nature along with my shameless ability to divulge very intimate details of my life to everyone, gives people a sense that they actually know me, that they actually know what is going on inside my brain. The truth is, nobody actually knows because I make sure of it. This is how I want it.

I have never been diagnosed with manic depression or anxiety because I never needed someone else to validate the issues I knew I already had. What were they going to do for me? Prescribe me medication that I know will only make these issues seem worse? Enter me into a database so the government can label me as something I am not? This idea of numbing myself to the reality of my existence made me feel worse than the crippling depression that would prevent me from getting out of bed some mornings. Thus, I attempted to at least learn from these moments in time and then attempt to turn them into opportunities for growth.

A part of me believes that depression is simply a part of being human. That, if channeled correctly, actually teaches one about themselves. To me, being happy is like winning and being sad is like losing. If we learn more from our defeats than we do from our accomplishments, then there has to be validity to this idea that depression can be beneficial. I tend to recluse myself when depressed. I push everybody far enough away to leave me alone but not far enough to abandon me. I put myself up away, either in a room or place, and wallow in self-misery. This is my process, it is not a very good one, but it is my process none the less.

Some fits of depression last only half-a-day while others have lasted for weeks and even months. The initial feeling of having no control over myself turned out to be the only thing I could actually control. I allow myself to fall victim to the paradox of attempting to control external factors while disallowing myself to control my internal factors; that being my thoughts, my actions, and my mindset.

If you noticed the subtle switch from past to present tense in that last paragraph, there is reason for that. I am not immune to fits of mania induced depression. I fall victim to bouts of anxiety. They still come quite frequently. As I am writing these words, I am having one of those days. One of those days where everything seems fruitless, everything seems hopeless and uncontrollable. Today is also one of those days where the darkness graces me with a hospitable familiarity as it begins to consume the outskirts of my mind. In a day that I feel so very alone, I find masochistic comfort in the depression which is beginning to hold me close once more.

To me these are the hardest emotional chains to break away from. The depression has always been there with me therefore, it has become a part of me. When mentally self-destructing, it is the only part of me that I allow as company. I already pushed those who can help far enough away. I am now out of their sight as well as out of their mind, just how I want it. I have disconnected. I have gained the isolation I so desperately seek. I now sit here, allowing the familiar feeling of self-loathing overtake me. This causational self-pity allows me to adopt a self-defeated attitude, a victim-hood mentality. In a way I am home, in another way I am completely alone. This is sort of what I want.

In my loneliness, the feedback loop of self-deprecating thought is omnipresent. It starts with one feeling, a feeling of self-insufficiency; like I am not good enough, strong enough, smart enough, experienced enough, or even deserving of some existential circumstance.

This is the darkness reaching out its hand, beckoning me to come with it down the shadowed road once more like every time before. The next thought is guilt-ridden and anxious. I spent so much energy trying to avoid this road with my familiar enemy yet find myself, once more, trekking its path. Internally, my feet keep moving yet my mind wants to go no further.

The darkness begins to console me, tell me I am validated in my thoughts and feelings, coercing me into believing that I need to walk this path. This makes me angry. Angry that I feel guilty. Angry at the feeling of self-insufficiency. Angry that I feel guilty about feeling self-insufficient. The darkness then wraps its arms around me. I continue this vicious, self-defecated thought process of victimhood. I feel validated in my feeling of self-insufficiency yet mad at myself that I am no longer strong enough to realize what it is, Bullshit. I am now self-aware of the fact that what I am feeling is bullshit.

This pisses me off even more than feeling angry for feeling self-insufficient. Tears begin to well in the corners of my eyes. Anger-laden depression all based in my own psychological prison which I have created out of thin air. My Rabbit Hole. My familiar place. Warm, comfortable, just how I left it. Not at-all what I want anymore. It demonstrates the darkness swooning us into its clutches.

The darkness lays me down to sleep. Tucks me into bed. Stuffs the sheets under my body and pulls the blanket close to my neck. The only companion I have in this self-manifested destruction of mind and worth. It may not be good for me but it is there for me none the less.

When nobody else is there for me, the darkness cuddles me like a mother does her infant. I’ve already pushed people away, not wanting them to be dragged into my Bullshit, yet hating myself for once more allowing it to consume me. I am now self-hating for allowing myself to fall in manic love all-over again with the darkness. like a bad ex-companion, I am smitten. Like quicksand, I am stuck. This is a scary place, this was not where I ever wanted to be again.

I finally reach my limit, I can no longer do it alone. I reach out. I reach out to the very people I wanted to keep away. They lend out a warm hand. They untuck me from my proverbial bed, pick me up, and walk me back into the light. Through conversing and listening I find my self-worth again. Through acknowledging the actual facts from the manifested mania, I remember who I am and what I am about. The darkness slips back into the forest. The sunlight hits my face. I take a deep breath of fresh air. I am okay. This time at least, I am okay.
